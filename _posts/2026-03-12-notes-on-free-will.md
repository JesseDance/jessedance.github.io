# One Box, or Two?

Today, I was reminded of a certain thought experiment that appears to be straight-forward at first, then a little complicated, then obvious again after some thought. This experiment is known as Newcome's Paradox. 

If you aren't familiar, here is the setup:

There are two boxes in a room, A and B.

Box A has something like $1,000 dollars in it, and if you were in the room you could clearly see the money inside.

Box B is different. Box B is closed and you can't see inside it. For the sake of the experiment, we will assume that there is either $1,000,000 dollars inside or $0 dollars inside, and the pivotal detail is that you don't know what's inside Box B.

There is also a super smart guy, let's call him Jerry. Jerry may even be some kind of super intelligent AI. You are told that Jerry is very good at guessing what will happen in the future, so good at guessing what will happen, that Jerry has even made a guess at what you will do after you are given the choice that is part of this experiment.

Eventually, the choice is presented to you. You can walk into the room with the boxes of money (either $1,000 or $1,001,00 total). You can have the money in the boxes, but you must choose whether you get the money from both boxes or just the mystery box (Box B). 

So just choose both boxes and get all that money right? Not so fast. You probably guessed that it's more complicated than that, and the greedy option has some kind of catch.

Jerry might have changed what was in Box B. If Jerry thinks you are going to be greedy and take both boxes. Jerry will take the money from Box B and there will be nothing inside, but if Jerry thinks you are going to take the chance with only the mystery box, Jerry will leave the $1,000,000 in the box for you.

Why is this even a dilemma? It seems like you are just getting either $1,000 or $1,000,000. So just choose to only take Box B and walk away with a potential $1,000,000 right?

The question that lies underneath the surface and ends up tempting some to consider taking both boxes is whether you can truly change your mind at the last second. Remember that Jerry has made a prediction BEFORE you have made the choice. So you can go into the room with the full intent to take only Box B, and this means that Jerry left the money in the box. Now, if the money is already in the box when you walked into the room, there is nothing stopping you from just taking the extra $1,000 dollars.

This is the line of thought that creates the paradox. The easy choice is that you should temper your greed and aim to only take the mystery box. The difficult choice is to consider that you either have the agency to take that extra $1,000 dollars or you accept the reality that Jerry has probably guessed correctly and the implications this has on your own agency and free will.

Unfortunately, even the way I have described the problem so far has likely influenced your view a bit, but I invite you to take a moment and consider what you would do anyway. Is there an obvious answer? Do you think anyone else that is thinking rationally would arrive at the same conclusion?
